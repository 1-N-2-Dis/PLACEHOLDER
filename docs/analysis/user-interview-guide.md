# User Interview Guide — validating GuidHer with real PUP women

> **Owner:** Alex. **Companion to** the probe sheet in [alex-pitch-kit.md §5](./alex-pitch-kit.md)
> (the 10 confirm-or-kill questions) — this doc is the *how* around those questions: the method, the
> ethics, who to talk to, how to recruit, and how to log. Do not restate rubric weights (owned by
> [00-hackathon-context.md](../00-hackathon-context.md)) or business rules (owned by
> [03-prd.md](../03-prd.md)); this doc links to them.
>
> **Why this exists:** "Is the problem validated?" is the judges' first question and our weakest
> point ([hackathon context §7](../00-hackathon-context.md)). Desk research is not validation. One
> real quote from one real PUP woman beats every statistic in the register. This is how we get it —
> **ethically, because we're asking women about commute safety, a sensitive topic.**
>
> **Timeline reality:** the final is **July 9**. From today that's ~3 days. This guide is built for
> *few, fast, high-quality* interviews (target ≥3, aim 5), not a research programme.

## 0. The one thing to internalize first

You are **not** here to show the app. You are here to learn how she *actually* commutes tonight and
what she *already does* about it. The product stays hidden until the very end (reasons in §3). If you
walk out with "she said she'd use it," you have nothing. If you walk out with "last Tuesday she paid
₱50 for a trike she couldn't afford just to skip a dark stretch, and she'd already asked her group
chat if anyone was near Pureza" — you have validation.

## 1. Interviewing principles — The Mom Test (the method)

From Rob Fitzpatrick's *The Mom Test* (2013) — the standard for customer discovery. Three rules,
[widely summarized](https://www.momtestbook.com/) as:

1. **Talk about her life, not your idea.** Never ask "would you use an app that…". Ask what she did,
   when, and what it cost her. Content rephrased for licensing compliance.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not opinions about the future.** "Would you…" and "do you
   usually…" invite polite fiction. "Walk me through *last night*" gets truth. People are unreliable
   narrators of their future selves and reliable reporters of last Tuesday.
3. **Talk less, listen more.** If you're talking, you're not learning. Aim for ~20% you, 80% her.

Practical tells, from the same method:
- **Compliments are worthless data.** "That's a great idea" / "I'd totally use that" = a warning
  you've started pitching. Steer back to her life.
- **Chase the specific, dodge the generic.** "I always feel unsafe there" → "Tell me about the last
  time. What happened? What did you do next?"
- **Look for what she's already *doing* and *paying*.** Behavior and money are signal; opinions and
  hypotheticals are noise. (This is exactly the `said / did / paid` tiering in the
  [evidence register](../evidence-register.md).)
- **Dig into emotion and workarounds.** When she gets animated or describes a hack (walks fast, bag
  on chest, waits for a kasabay, messages the group chat), you've found a real problem. Mine it.

## 2. The ethical layer — interviewing women about safety (non-negotiable)

Commute-safety talk will sometimes surface harassment, following, groping, or worse — the
[evidence register](../updated-evidence-register.md) already documents these on the zone. That makes
this **sensitive research**, not a casual chat. Adapted (proportionately — we are asking about
commuting, not conducting a violence survey) from the WHO-based
["six golden principles for interviewing women who may have experienced violence"](https://www.svri.org/six-golden-principles-for-interviewing-women-who-may-have-experienced-violence/)
(Dr. Henriette Jansen, UNFPA kNOwVAWdata, Asia-Pacific) and
[trauma-informed qualitative research guidelines](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/14780887.2022.2107967).
Content rephrased from public sources for licensing compliance.

1. **A woman interviews women.** Same-gender interviewer materially improves disclosure and comfort.
   On our team that means **Helena or Farhana leads the sensitive parts**; Alex can organize/observe
   if the participant is comfortable, but should not be the sole interviewer for a 1:1 on this topic.
2. **Consent, and make leaving easy.** Say up front: this is for a student project, it's voluntary,
   she can skip any question or stop anytime, and nothing is shared with her name attached. Get a
   clear "yes" before starting and before any recording.
3. **Privacy and confidentiality.** Talk one-on-one, in private (a quiet campus spot or a private
   DM/video call — never a public FB comment thread; see §6). Store notes de-identified: "2nd-year,
   commutes Pureza line" — not her name next to the incident.
4. **Do not push; you are not a counsellor.** If she gets upset or goes quiet, slow down, let her
   pass, or stop. Never press for details of a painful event to get a better quote. A good interview
   never costs the participant anything.
5. **Have a referral ready.** If someone discloses a recent assault or ongoing danger, don't play
   rescuer — hand her real options. Keep this line ready: **PNP Aleng Pulis / Women and Children
   Protection hotline, the PUP Guidance and Counseling Office, and national emergency 911.** *(Team:
   confirm the exact current PUP office contact before interviews — do not invent a number.)*
   > This is basic human decency in the room. It does **not** conflict with **BR-002 (no rescue/SOS
   > in the product)** — BR-002 governs what the *app* promises, not how *you* treat a person who
   > trusts you with something hard.
6. **Mind your own head.** Hearing these stories back-to-back is heavy. Debrief with each other
   after; don't stack five interviews in one evening.

## 3. Why the product stays hidden until the end (and how to reveal it)

This was your open question. The answer, reinforced by both the method and Ate Gianne's advice:

- **During the interview: hide it.** Opening with the app contaminates everything — she'll rate
  *you*, be polite, and give a fake positive ("ay, ang ganda, gagamitin ko 'to"). That's noise. You
  can't un-see her reaction once she's seen the app, so ask about her real behavior *first*.
- **At the very end: reveal it, then shut up and watch.** After the questions, show one screen and
  say nothing leading — "we're building this; what do you think it's for?" Her unprompted read is a
  bonus signal (does she even understand it?). Reaction-to-a-thing is weak data, so treat it as a
  tiebreaker, never as the validation itself.
- **In recruiting posts: a screenshot is fine.** A picture stops the scroll (Ate Gianne) and doubles
  as traction. That's marketing, not validation — different job, different rules. Just never rely on
  "people liked the post" as evidence the problem is real.

## 4. Interview structure (a 15–20 minute arc)

Keep it short; respect her time. The numbered questions live in
[pitch kit §5](./alex-pitch-kit.md) — don't re-read them robotically, *hold the arc* and let them
come up naturally.

| Phase | ~time | Goal | What you do |
|-------|------:|------|-------------|
| **Open / consent** | 2 min | Comfort + permission | Warm intro, the §2.2 consent lines, ask to take notes. No app, no pitch. |
| **Her last commute** | 5 min | Real behavior | "Walk me through your commute *last night*, door to door." Follow the legs. (pitch kit Q1–Q3) |
| **The decision + the group chat** | 5 min | **The riskiest assumption** | How did she decide her route *before* leaving? Does she ask a group chat / friends? Get her to *show you* a real message. (Q4–Q5) — this is the single most important 5 minutes. |
| **The conditions** | 3 min | Which segments/conditions matter | Where exactly felt off, and *why* (dark? empty? a past incident?). Let her name the spots; only prompt specific ones if she doesn't (§5). (Q2, Q6) |
| **Contribution + return** | 2 min | Will she give back + come back | Would she tap once to flag a dark/empty stretch? What would bring her back next week? (Q7, Q10) |
| **Reveal + wrap** | 2 min | Bonus reaction + thanks | Show one screen (§3), thank her, ask if you can follow up, offer to share what you learn. |

**Language discipline in the room (BR-001):** you're mapping *fixable conditions* — lighting, crowd,
recent condition — never "dangerous areas" or crime labels. If she says "that's a dangerous place,"
that's her words (log them), but your follow-up probes the *condition*: "what makes it feel that
way — the lighting? how empty it is?" You're learning what the product can actually represent.

## 5. Which segments/conditions to validate? (your strategic question)

First, a reframe you need before you post anything: **you are not validating "danger zones."** That
phrasing is exactly what **[BR-001](../03-prd.md)** forbids and what makes a judge call this a crime
map. You are validating two things, in this priority order:

1. **The behavior (highest value, location-independent):** does she consult a guide / group chat
   before leaving, and would she contribute? If this is real, GuidHer has a reason to exist. If it's
   not, no pin matters. **Spend your best energy here.**
2. **The conditions on specific segments (second):** and here, pick by leverage, not by severity.

**Validate the *common, behavior-changing* conditions — not the rare, severe ones.** Reasoning:
- GuidHer is a *pre-trip conditions* guide. Its value is the everyday reroute decision (a dark, empty
  stretch she avoids nightly), not the once-a-year armed incident. A confirmed "I reroute around the
  unlit part of Teresa every night" changes the product; "someone got robbed at knifepoint once"
  does not, and it drags you toward crime-map framing (BR-001/BR-007).
- So: **common + makes her change behavior + inside our actual coverage zone.**

**Concretely, prioritize the core-zone P1 segments already queued in
[updated-evidence-register.md §4](../updated-evidence-register.md)** — but let *her* raise them; only
prompt if she doesn't:

| Priority | Segment | What a real confirmation settles |
|----------|---------|----------------------------------|
| **P1** | **Teresa Street** (PUP → Pureza artery) | Our #1 target street; still partly desk-research. Which stretch, what time, what condition. |
| **P1** | **Pureza approaches + trike group-forming** | The strongest *behavioral* signal we have — women already group up to avoid predatory fares. Confirm it's real and current. |
| **P1** | **Legarda pickpocket / dark exit** | Confirms how organized the everyday risk is and what she already does. |
| **P1** | **Recto–Legarda late-night walk vs. paid ride** | Tests whether a *time-aware* reroute is something she'd actually use. |

Don't spread ≥3 interviews across 21 locations. Go deep on the core zone. If she doesn't commute
these, follow *her* segments — a real segment she uses beats a "priority" one she's never walked.

> Promote a seed pin to **validated** only when a real commuter confirms **segment + condition +
> behavior** (per the [evidence register](../evidence-register.md) rule). Log the quote verbatim.

## 6. Reaching PUP women — recruiting strategy

Three channels, in order of yield. Ate Gianne's advice (GDG chief marketing) is folded in and marked.

**A. Your own network first (fastest, warmest).** Blockmates, orgs, friends-of-friends who are PUP
women commuting the zone. One warm intro > ten cold posts. Ask them, then ask *them* for one referral
each ("sino pa kaya sa block mo?"). This alone can hit ≥3 in a day.

**B. Women's / advocacy orgs and PUP student orgs (high-quality, aligned).** *(Ate Gianne)* Email or
privately message PUP GAD-adjacent orgs, women's collectives, and college councils. Frame it as
*"we're looking for participants for user interviews as part of a student project"* — not a promo.
They're advocacy-aligned; they'll often help. This also seeds your GAD relationship for the pitch.

**C. PUP Facebook groups (widest reach + traction).** r/PUPians-style FB groups, college/section
groups. This is the post in §7. *(Ate Gianne)*: lead with a relatable feeling, not the app; add a
screenshot so it doesn't get scrolled past; keep it conversational-professional; make it about
*learning from their commute*, not selling.

**Golden rule across all three (Ate Gianne + §2):** don't open by explaining the app. Open by asking
about *their* commute. It's an interview, not a demo — that keeps the conversation natural and keeps
you out of fake-positive territory.

## 8. Form vs. comments — which, and why (your other question)

**Recommendation: use a short Google Form as the private capture net, but don't make it a wall — also
let them comment/DM, then you reach out privately. Never collect the actual safety details in public
comments.**

Why this specific combination:

- **Public comments are the wrong place for the substance — on privacy grounds, not just conversion.**
  Asking a woman to *publicly* type that she feels unsafe on a specific street ties her identity +
  location + vulnerability together in an open group. That's a real safety/privacy harm, and it's the
  first thing every VAW research ethic ([WHO/SVRI](https://www.svri.org/six-golden-principles-for-interviewing-women-who-may-have-experienced-violence/),
  [World Bank](https://www.worldbank.org/en/programs/violence-against-women-and-girls/ethics))
  protects against: keep it confidential, keep it private. Public comments also give lower-quality,
  performative answers. So: comments are fine for *"me, I'm in"* — never for the safety details.
- **A Google Form is the right capture tool** because it's private, it *qualifies* (2–3 screening Qs:
  Do you commute the PUP–Sta. Mesa zone? After dark? Willing to a 15-min chat?), it captures a
  contact (FB or email) once, and it reads as organized and professional (Ate Gianne). Cost: every
  extra field loses people — so keep it to **4 fields max**.
- **But don't force the form** — a form as the *only* door adds friction and kills reach. Let the
  low-effort "comment 'me' / DM us" be the top of the funnel; the form is where the willing self-
  select and hand you a contact. Comments/DMs also make the post itself perform better (engagement =
  reach = more eyes = traction for the pitch).

**The funnel, concretely:**

```
Public FB post (relatable caption + screenshot)
   │
   ├─ "comment 'me' / DM us"   ← lowest friction, max reach + traction
   │        │
   │        └─ you reply privately → send the form or go straight to scheduling
   │
   └─ short Google Form link   ← qualifies + captures contact in one step
            │
            └─ you DM/email to schedule the private 15-min interview
```

**The form, minimal (4 fields):**
1. Are you a woman who commutes the PUP Sta. Mesa zone, usually after evening classes? (Yes/No)
2. Which parts of the commute do you usually take? (short text — jeep/LRT/walk, rough route)
3. Up for a private 15-min chat about your commute? (Yes / Maybe / No)
4. Where can we reach you? (FB link or email)

> Keep the form's intro one sentence, same framing as the post: *learning from your commute, private,
> anonymous in any write-up.* Don't describe the app on the form either.

## 9. Logging (so it counts as evidence)

- **Write answers verbatim, same day** (memory decays fast). De-identify: "3rd-year, Anonas jeep +
  Pureza LRT" — never her name beside an incident.
- Tag each confirmed item `said / did / paid` and map it back to the
  [evidence register](../evidence-register.md); promote a seed pin only on segment + condition +
  behavior confirmation.
- **Pull one quote for the pitch.** "A 2nd-year rider told us she already messages her block GC 'safe
  ba dyan?' before every night commute" is the single highest-leverage sentence you can put in front
  of a judge. That quote is the whole point of this exercise.

## 10. Definition of done (for this validation sprint)

- [ ] ≥3 (aim 5) real PUP women interviewed, product hidden until the end, notes logged verbatim.
- [ ] The riskiest assumption tested: do they consult a guide/GC before leaving, and would they
      contribute? (a clear yes *or* a clear no — both are useful).
- [ ] ≥1 core-zone segment's *condition + behavior* confirmed by a real commuter.
- [ ] ≥1 verbatim quote ready for the pitch and the Q&A "have you validated the problem?" answer.
- [ ] Every interaction stayed private, consented, and conditions-only (BR-001) — no public safety
      disclosures, no rescue promises (BR-002).
