# GuidHer User Interview Findings
### Consolidated analysis of first-party commuter interviews

> **Owner:** Alex (validation). Companion to the [User Interview Guide](./user-interview-guide.md)
> (method + ethics) and [Pitch Kit §5](./alex-pitch-kit.md) (probe sheet). Feeds the "have you
> validated the problem?" answer in the [Pitch Kit §4](./alex-pitch-kit.md) Q&A map. Conditions-only
> discipline (BR-001) and no-rescue (BR-002) per [PRD](../03-prd.md).
>
> **Interviews analyzed:** 7 (Mary Jean, Neil, John Christian, Elsa, Eden, Myrine, VJ).
> **Method:** semi-structured chat interviews; product hidden until the end, then revealed for reaction.
>
> **Evidence weighting.** The strongest evidence here is reported past *behavior* (traveling with a
> kasabay, paying to skip a stretch, asking friends, staying on high alert). The weakest is the
> "I would use your app" reactions gathered *after* the reveal — treat those as supporting color,
> not proof (Mom Test discipline, see interview guide).
>
> **Privacy note.** Names are kept for this internal record only. For anything public or
> judge-facing, de-identify (e.g. "2nd-year PUP commuter, Teresa St line"). Never attach a real name
> to a safety disclosure in a shared document.

## Part 2. Interviewee profiles (detail preserved)

**Mary Jean — PUP, after night class.**
Reads conditions at the street level. Teresa St felt safe: "maraming ilaw, maraming tao, and maraming open na establishments so I feel at ease." Around Anonas and the looban streets it flips: "madilim yung paligid and medjo tahimik... mostly puro households," where she raises her situational awareness. Pureza felt safe "kasi 24/7 open yung ibang establishments." She stays alert regardless: "pati kami kapag gabi na di parin nawawala yung pagka alert." At night she sticks to the main route "sa maraming tao" and avoids the looban "kasi madilim and walang street lights." She decides her route through friends. She would report in one tap. On the reveal: "I'll open this! Goods na goods siya esp if galing kang PUP and then kakatapos lang ng night class." Suggestion: a page listing emergency hotlines and contacts around Sta. Mesa. On a forum she was unsure.

**Neil — UST, España and Recto corridor.**
First-year schedule was 7-to-7, so he went home at night. Rides a jeep around Recto as a last resort, and often asks around "kung may mga pauwi palang ba or malapit so we can go home tgt." He names the risk plainly: "madilim kasi tlg sa ibang spots near ust papuntang recto and maraming cases na ng criminal activities." His workarounds cost money: books Angkas even if "mapapamahal," or takes LRT but still passes Bambang "which is delikado parin." His preferred safer option is to go with a blockmate to Lacson, then jeep from UST to Tayuman, "kahit dagdag gastos." Route knowledge came from parents (avoid Recto and dangerous areas) plus what he learned traveling with trusted friends. He would report to help someone feel safer. He would actively use the app if finished. He thinks a forum would help, so commuters can add personal experience even when an area is nominally safe. His one complaint was the AI art. On price he is frank: he rarely pays for subscriptions, ₱49 "seems fine," but he would rather it be free and accessible, funded by ads if needed.

**John Christian — far from PUP, Pasig-Quiapo route, past midnight, he/him, LGBTQIA+.**
Travels alone by choice, letting friends ride first. Complacency has set in from routine, but fear returns "lalo na pag nasa around v. mapa na yung area," especially with drunk, quiet passengers on the jeep, at which point he considers getting off and booking Move It. He asks friends to ride with him when they are also alone, splitting off "until we get to a safer midway." He changed his habitual route to a safer one (now alights at Shaw). He describes constant "high alert mode most of the time." He raises a vulnerability none of the others did: "lalo na pag nasa lgbtqia++ community ka, people deem u as weak, or worse, baka bigla kang maka-encounter ng homophobic and conventional remarks sa daan kahit you're just wanting to get home." He would report in one tap. On the reveal he would open it for alerts before leaving, and gave the most technical feedback: calibrate the route tracker, offer route options like Google or Apple Maps, and consider sakay.ph as a reference for commute routes. He wants the option to pick another route "if my current choice of route has a dangerously low score." He finds a forum useful because "knowing why someone decides to report something makes up for the deciding factor," while warning to "lagi tayong maging wary sa Data Privacy Act." He is bullish on an SOS feature: emergency contacts, location and route sharing, and even a field for the plate number or make of the vehicle "so when the time comes, it would take away minutes of survival being found by SOS."

**Elsa — intern in Quezon City, lives in San Jose, Bulacan.**
Her commute is 2-3 hours. She leaves the office by 6PM for a bus to Fairview. Usually uneventful but physically hard: "sobrang siksikan," hot even with aircon, standing room, mixed smells. Her worry rises "kapag I travel 9Pm onwards iba na kasi ugali ng mga tao." She once traveled at 12AM with a drunk, noisy crowd. She did not consider a different route "since they're both hassle anyway," but wishes she could leave earlier, around 4PM, which is not feasible because events run at night. She wishes she had a kasama, but "most hindi naman taga bulacan." She would report "if it's just one tap." She asked "does it come with an app?" and on hearing yes said "I'll definitely use it." Her suggestion: a comment section on community reports so people can ask the reporter for details. On price: "yeah specially if it's helpful for night commutes."

**Eden — office worker, relies on TNVS.**
When there is no more public transport, she books Grab or a TNVS for door-to-door "di na nakakastress na maglalakad pa." She leaves the office with co-workers, then separates. She waits for rides where there are many people and does not wait too late, because "mnsn feeling ko may tao sa likod ko na prang may something." She would report "if madali lang nmn to report since may data nmn." On the reveal: "Nice I already check it and it is helpful too especially kpag maulan," and she asked whether the conditions list would grow. She wants coverage of safe spots. Willingness to pay was not directly answered.

**Myrine — from Cavite, unfamiliar with Manila's LRT.**
Her most recent night commute was from a hackathon near Opus Mall, before 9PM, rushing to catch the last train without knowing the closing time. The only routes she knew were Angkas and Grab. Ortigas station felt scary and put her "full alert." Panic over the last train "dumoble na pagod ko." At EDSA station there was no ATM, so she crossed a high Pasay footbridge repeatedly, "madilim and they all had their focus sa sarili nila, madaling magkaroon ng salisi or dukutan." Withdrawing cash in Pasay felt unsafe, "madilim siya, medyo tago at walang bantay." She has not traveled alone in Manila late at night since. She wished she had taken a route she understood ("culture shock"), and she had a companion that trip. She would report if she encountered something in the future, though she worried her own knowledge is stale. On specific zones she flagged LRT-2 Pureza as "uncrowded... baka manakawan."

**VJ — Google Maps user, often gets home late, speaks for "saming girls."**
Her group planned to go home together from the start and checked in with each other before leaving: "ask each other kung ok na ba ang lahat." They had thoughts of "dapat 9 umalis na." Plan B was the carousel and MRT, same way home for all, and the last resort was to soft-commute by Angkas. She would report and "will also encourage my friends din." On the reveal she noted the app is partially finished and that the backend was not rendering, correctly recalling the "21 locations... scraped through forums, live reports, and credible sources." She said "a few polishing nalang then good to go na." As a heavy Google Maps user she would use GuidHer, especially when gabihin or on unfamiliar routes, and valued that "your project also provide information such as the risk summary and incident heatmap... especially saming girls lalo na't delikado kapag gabi." Her suggested feature: highlight nearby police stations and their contact information (the team noted this is already being implemented). On price: maybe, if more features are added. She also confirmed the project context and named teammates Helena, Farhana, and Jim.

## Part 3. Cross-cutting themes

**1. Hypervigilance is universal.** Every interviewee described staying on alert at night, in near-identical words: "di parin nawawala yung pagka alert" (Mary Jean), "high alert mode most of the time" (John), "full alert" (Myrine), rising worry after 9PM (Elsa), the feeling of "may tao sa likod ko" (Eden). This is the emotional core of the problem and the strongest shared finding.

**2. Traveling with a kasabay is the default safety behavior.** Mary Jean, Neil, John, and VJ all coordinate company for the trip home, and Elsa and Myrine wished they could. VJ's group even formalized it ("sabay-sabay ding uuwi"). This confirms the group-forming behavior the evidence register called the highest-value signal, now in first-party voice.

**3. Route knowledge comes from people, never from a tool.** Friends (Mary Jean, Neil, John), parents (Neil), and lived experience are the sources. Not one interviewee used a safety app, and the Google Maps users use tools built for speed, not safety. This is the clearest validation of the gap GuidHer fills.

**4. Paid avoidance is real.** Neil pays more for Angkas or a longer blockmate route "kahit dagdag gastos." John books Move It to escape a bad jeep. Eden defaults to Grab or a TNVS. This is the "paid" evidence tier the register marked as not met — now met for the underlying behavior, though not yet as willingness to pay for the information product itself.

**5. The conditions vocabulary matches the product, and adds one.** Users spontaneously described poor lighting, low crowd, and open-versus-closed establishments, which map directly to the condition model. Eden introduced a new one: rain ("helpful especially kpag maulan"). Weather belongs in the conditions picture.

**6. The LGBTQIA+ risk is distinct and real.** John's account of being "deemed weak" and facing homophobic remarks on the way home validates the secondary persona with a first-hand voice, and strengthens the case for framing GuidHer around women and LGBTQIA+ riders together.

**7. Willingness to pay is soft.** Only Elsa gave a clear yes to ₱49/mo. Neil prefers free with ads for accessibility, VJ would pay only for more features, and the rest did not commit. Note that ads were already rejected in the business model on trust grounds, so Neil's suggestion conflicts with a prior decision. The honest read: users resist paying, which supports free-to-users funded by institutions.

| Request | Who asked | Reconciliation with product rules |

| Native app, not just web | Elsa, Eden | Users expect a phone app. Consider a PWA so it installs without app-store overhead. |
| Design polish, AI art disliked | Neil | Minor but noted. Aligns with the move away from decorative or emoji-heavy UI. |

## Part 6. Validation scorecard

- **Riskiest assumption (will she consult a guide before leaving, and contribute?):** Supported. Users already consult friends and family, the gap is the absence of a tool, all seven would report in one tap, and most would open GuidHer before a trip. Caveat: these are stated intentions gathered after the reveal, not yet observed retention.
- **Problem is real:** Strongly supported by consistent past behavior across seven independent commuters.
- **Paid-avoidance behavior:** Confirmed (Angkas, Grab, Move It, longer paid routes).
- **Willingness to pay for the app:** Weak. Points to the institution-pays model.
- **Conditions-only framing:** Holds, and users speak the same vocabulary. Add rain.
- **LGBTQIA+ persona:** Validated by a first-hand account.

## Part 7. Suggestions

**Use in the pitch (validation and "who pays"):**
- Lead the problem beat with a behavior quote, not a statistic. Mary Jean's "di parin nawawala yung pagka alert" or John's "high alert mode most of the time" lands harder than the 88% figure, then the figure backs it up.
- Answer "have you validated the problem?" with the behavior cluster: kasabay coordination, paid avoidance, and route knowledge that comes only from people. Seven interviews, one consistent story.
- Answer "who pays?" honestly with the weak willingness to pay, which is why users are free and institutions fund it.
- Use VJ's line for the trust/relevance beat: "mapapanatag loob ko if I check it before ako lalarga... especially saming girls."

**Product decisions to record (log in POSTMORTEM):**
- Reaffirm the no-open-forum ruling, but note the real user demand and adopt structured corroboration plus an emergency-contacts page as the answer to that demand.
- Draw the SOS boundary explicitly: informational contacts yes, dispatch or rescue no (BR-002). This is now a documented user request, so the decision should be documented too.
- Approve an emergency-hotlines page and nearby-police-contacts layer as informational features that satisfy demand without breaking BR-002.

**Data and build:**
- Correct the Teresa Street seed to segment-level conditions (main lit, looban dark). Add Anonas looban, near-UST-to-Recto, Bambang, and V. Mapa as user-reported dark or uneasy conditions, tagged as first-party.
- Add rain as a condition or context field (Eden).
- Prioritize route-tracker calibration before any real deployment (John, VJ).
- Consider a PWA so the "does it come with an app?" expectation is met cheaply.

**Ethics and methodology:**
- Add a Data Privacy Act consent point, which a user (John) explicitly raised and the mentor also recommended.
- De-identify these quotes before any external use.
- Get one clean "paid" quote formally on record for the evidence register (Neil or Eden qualifies).
- Next step: barangays are the only outreach group left, plus follow-ups with these seven, several of whom offered to stay in touch.
